# Wealthsimple Is Eating Canadian Banking. The Big Six Aren't Ready.

The Canadian personal banking market is undergoing a structural shift that the Big Six banks are publicly ignoring and privately scrambling to contain. Wealthsimple — once dismissed as a robo-advisor for millennials — now manages over C$100 billion in assets, serves 3 million Canadians, and is profitable. Its fastest-growing segment is clients with over $1 million in assets. The Big Six's collective response has been half-measures and silence.

This post maps the competitive landscape: where Wealthsimple is winning, which client segments are at risk, how much money is on the table, what strategies could work, and what the banks are actually doing about it.

## The Scale of the Shift

Wealthsimple's AUA nearly doubled from roughly C$64 billion at end of 2024 to over C$100 billion by October 2025 — hitting that milestone three years ahead of their own target. In October 2025 they raised C$750 million at a C$10 billion valuation, co-led by Dragoneer and Singapore's GIC, with CPP Investments also participating. They reached profitability in 2024 and maintained it through 2025.

Usage among Canadians went from 2% in 2019 to 14% in 2025 — a sevenfold increase in six years. According to the Environics Research Financial Services Switching Study (Canada's longest-running, over 20 years), 24% of Canadians switched a financial account or product in 2025 — an all-time high. Online switching became the primary channel (55%, up from 49%), and 49% of switchers cited high prices as a factor. Wealthsimple led the market with record-setting switching-driven growth across multiple product lines.

The user profile has also matured. In 2019, Wealthsimple users were early adopters driven by novelty and frustration with traditional banking. By 2025, the Environics analysis shows the base is now defined by ambition, agency, and personal confidence rather than rebellion. It's no longer just tech-savvy millennials. It's mainstream.

## The Product Expansion

Wealthsimple's strategy has been to systematically fill every gap that used to keep people anchored to a Big Five bank:

- Zero-commission stock, ETF, and options trading
- Chequing and savings accounts (renamed from "Cash" accounts)
- Crypto trading
- Tax filing
- Managed portfolios and direct indexing
- Visa Infinite credit card (launched June 2025)
- Cash deposits via Canada Post (January 2026)
- CIRO approval for futures and forecast contracts (March 2026)

CEO Michael Katchen has stated explicitly that the goal is to become Canada's largest financial institution. That sounds like bluster until you look at the trajectory.

## Regulatory Tailwinds

Two regulatory shifts are making this worse for incumbents:

**Open Banking.** Canada's Open Banking framework launched Phase 1 (read access) in October 2025, giving fintechs and credit unions direct access to Payments Canada's rails for the first time. Phase 2 (write access — account switching and payment initiation) is targeted for mid-2027. Once Phase 2 is live, customers won't need to formally "move" to Wealthsimple — they can connect their bank data and gradually shift activity over, product by product, without closing their bank account.

**Transfer fee ban.** The 2025 federal budget announced a ban on investment account transfer-out fees, being implemented in 2026. That $150+ transfer fee was one of the banks' most effective retention tools. Once it's gone, the friction cost of leaving a Big Five brokerage drops to near zero.

Meanwhile, Revolut — which captures roughly a third of new retail accounts in Europe — has hired a Canadian GM and is expected to re-enter Canada in 2026. The competitive environment is about to get more crowded.

## Where Banks Can't Win (And Should Stop Trying)

The core insight is that banks are trying to charge for commodity products — chequing, brokerage, basic savings — while their actual competitive advantage is in complex products: mortgages, lending, insurance, tax-integrated advice, and commercial banking.

On everyday banking and self-directed investing for financially literate Canadians, Wealthsimple is objectively better. Zero fees, clean app, integrated platform. A Big Five bank charging $9.95/trade and $30/month for a chequing account is effectively paying Wealthsimple's client acquisition cost.

The strategic question isn't "how do I keep 100% of this client's wallet?" — it's "how do I keep the high-margin products and make them sticky?"

## Where Banks Still Have a Moat

Wealthsimple doesn't offer mortgages, HELOCs, commercial lending, full-service wealth advisory, insurance, or business banking. Those products require a balance sheet, regulatory infrastructure, and human expertise that a fintech can't easily replicate.

The banks' moat isn't the chequing account — it's the C$800K mortgage that takes 25 years to pay off, the HELOC collateralized against your home, and the advisor who helps structure your estate. Every dollar spent defending $30/month chequing fees is a dollar not spent making the mortgage experience seamless.

## The Client-by-Client Breakdown

Not all clients are equally at risk. Here's how the competitive dynamics play out by segment, with order-of-magnitude asset estimates built from StatCan household wealth data and demographic counts. (Baseline: Canadian household net worth reached $18.4 trillion in Q3 2025, household financial assets at $11.7 trillion, roughly 16 million households.)

### Profile 1: "The Young Builder"
*25-30, tech/professional, $100-150k income, hates fees, does everything on phone*

**Assets at risk today: ~$25-40B** (~500-700k households, $30-80k investable each)

Wealthsimple is objectively better for this person right now. There's no rational reason for them to stay at a Big Five bank for personal banking and investing.

The only bank edge is the mortgage pre-approval relationship. This person will want to buy a condo in 2-3 years. The strategy that could work: a "future homeowner" pathway — no-fee chequing bundled with a mortgage pre-qualification that follows them, preferential terms when they're ready. Frame it as: "Wealthsimple can hold your TFSA, but they can't get you into a home."

Current assets are small, but lifetime revenue of this cohort is $90-150B. This is where the pipeline gets poisoned.

### Profile 2: "The Young Family"
*33, dual income $180k household, first kid, house, RESP needed*

**Assets at risk today: ~$120-200B** (~1.2-1.5M households, $60-180k investable each)

This is the single most contested segment. They opened a Wealthsimple account at 26 for the TFSA. Now they're wondering why they'd go anywhere else for the RESP.

But this is also where complexity starts to favour the bank. Mortgage, HELOC, RESP, life insurance, disability insurance, wills — Wealthsimple doesn't do any of that (yet). The play is bundled *advice* — not bundled products. Assign a dedicated advisor who proactively reaches out at life milestones. Waive chequing fees for mortgage holders. Make the RESP dead simple to set up in the same meeting.

Key risk: if the bank's "advisor" is just a product pusher selling 2% MER mutual funds, this family will figure it out and leave everything except the mortgage.

This cohort also holds roughly $500-700B in mortgage balances. Losing the deposit and investment relationship doesn't just cost $150B in AUA — it breaks the bundling model that makes Canadian retail banking profitable.

### Profile 3: "The Optimizer"
*38, $250k+ investable, financially literate, reads Canadian Couch Potato, posts on RedFlagDeals*

**Assets at risk today: ~$120-175B** (~300-500k households, median ~$350k)

This cohort is *already leaving*. They've calculated that bank brokerage fees compound to $26,000+ in lost growth over 20 years. Many have already moved to Wealthsimple, Questrade, or IBKR. The bank's recovery rate here is near zero on brokerage.

The honest strategy: let them go on brokerage. Stop burning advisor time trying to retain a self-directed investor who will never buy a mutual fund. Focus on keeping their banking relationship free and frictionless. The long game is re-engaging when their tax situation gets complex (stock options, rental property, incorporated business) and they need real financial planning.

### Profile 4: "The Affluent Professional"
*48, physician/lawyer/exec, $1M+ investable, incorporated, complex tax situation*

**Assets at risk today: ~$400-600B** (~250-400k households, median ~$1.5M including corporate)

This is the big one. Wealthsimple's fastest-growing segment is clients with over $1 million in assets. These people aren't moving for free trades — they're moving because they've realized their bank advisor is selling them 2% MER mutual funds that underperform a Wealthsimple balanced portfolio. The trust is broken.

Annual fee revenue at risk on $500B at 1% AUM: roughly **$5B/year** in wealth management fees. Losing 20% of this cohort would wipe out ~$1B/year in recurring fee income across the Big Five.

This is a talent war, not a product war. The bank needs genuinely CFP/CFA-level advisors, not branch-level planners reading a script. Transparent fees (1% AUM declining to 0.5% at scale). Demonstrable value: "here's how I saved you $40k in taxes this year through corporate structuring." If the advisor can show that, Wealthsimple can't touch it. If the advisor is just buying the same iShares ETFs and charging 1%, this client will eventually figure it out.

### Profile 5: "The Newcomer"
*30, immigrated from India/China/Philippines, professional credentials, $80k*

**Assets at risk today: ~$5-12B** (~200-300k professional/economic class households)

Negligible today. But a professional newcomer who builds a career and buys a house represents $1-2M in lifetime assets. At 250k households/year, that's a **$250-500B pipeline over 20 years**. Wealthsimple, KOHO, and Neo are all targeting this segment with frictionless onboarding.

The bank that earns trust early — by solving real pain points like international transfers and credit establishment, waiving fees for 2-3 years, not 12 months — locks in a 20-year relationship.

### Profile 6: "The Pre-Retiree"
*57, $800k in RRSPs, pension, starting to think about drawdown*

**Assets at risk today: ~$700B-1T** (~1.3-1.8M households, median ~$600k)

The banks' strongest fortress. These clients value stability, face-to-face advice, and institutional trust. Switching costs feel high at this life stage. Wealthsimple penetration is still low.

But the risk isn't zero — their adult kids are on Wealthsimple and influencing them. And 2.1% MER mutual fund statements raise questions.

The retention play is proactive retirement planning. Call this client at 55, not 65. Build a detailed drawdown plan (RRSP vs TFSA vs non-registered sequencing, OAS clawback optimization, pension bridge strategy). This is genuinely complex work that justifies fees and that Wealthsimple's robo can't replicate. But the fees need to come down — move them to a managed portfolio at 1-1.2% before their kid shows them what they're paying.

### Profile 7: "The Small Business Owner"
*42, owns a restaurant/clinic/e-commerce business, $300k personal + $200k corporate*

**Assets at risk: ~$250-400B personal + ~$150-250B business deposits** (~600-900k households)

The stickiest segment. Wealthsimple doesn't do business banking, payroll, merchant services, or commercial lending. The bundling here is genuine, not cosmetic. The risk is that personal investing gradually migrates to Wealthsimple while the business relationship stays — diluting cross-sell value but not breaking it.

Use the business relationship to own the personal relationship, not the other way around.

**Total investable assets at varying degrees of risk: ~$1.8-2.5 trillion** — roughly 15-20% of total Canadian household financial assets. Annual fee revenue at risk across all profiles: **~$13-18B**, a material chunk of the Big Five's combined non-interest income.

The segments where assets are largest (pre-retirees, affluent professionals) are still relatively defensible *if* banks deliver genuine advisory value. The segments where Wealthsimple is winning fastest (young builders, optimizers) represent smaller current assets but control the trajectory. Banks that let the pipeline go dry today will face a structurally smaller addressable market in 10 years.

## The Meta-Strategy

The winning strategy across all profiles comes down to one principle: **give away the commodities to keep the client in the ecosystem, and monetize the complexity.**

- Accept split-banking. The era of a single client having everything at one bank is ending. A growing number of Canadians will have chequing and self-directed investing at Wealthsimple and their mortgage, HELOC, and advisory wealth management at a Big Five bank.
- Double down on what Wealthsimple structurally can't do: mortgages, HELOCs, commercial lending, full-service wealth advisory, insurance, business banking.
- Stop punishing loyalty. Existing customers routinely get worse rates than new customers on mortgages, GICs, and savings. That asymmetry has always existed, but in a world where rate transparency is instant and switching friction is disappearing, it's suicidal.
- Fix the digital experience on the products you own. The mortgage renewal process at most Big Five banks is still terrible — a letter in the mail, a phone call, negotiation with someone who has less information than you do. If renewing your mortgage was as seamless as buying an ETF on Wealthsimple, clients would have one less reason to feel resentful.
- Use data advantages. A Big Five bank with a mortgage, credit card, payroll deposits, and savings can see a client's entire financial picture. That data should enable proactive, genuinely useful advice — not sales pitches.
- Consider a controlled fintech sub-brand. Rather than making RBC Direct Investing compete with Wealthsimple (risking exposing mass-market clients to options and crypto), create a separate digital brand with its own fee structure. Ring-fence the power tools there. Accept the cannibalization. The alternative is losing those clients entirely.

## What the Big Six Are Actually Doing

The answer is: not much, and none of them will say Wealthsimple's name out loud.

**RBC** — the most visible response, but still reactive. Launched GoSmart (mobile-first investing) in early 2026 and commission-free trading on select iShares ETFs in mid-2025. RedFlagDeals community reaction: "RBC are late to the party." Acquired mortgagetech Pinch Financial. At Investor Day 2025, the framing was "deepening client relationships" and "personalized advice" — classic bank language for "our edge is complexity, not simplicity." No public acknowledgment of client attrition. No pricing response on chequing fees. GoSmart is a separate app alongside existing RBC Direct Investing — fragmenting rather than unifying the experience.

**TD** — focused on U.S. AML remediation. Investor Day 2025 was consumed by the U.S. story. TD Easy Trade exists but is deliberately limited (50 free trades/year, phone-app only). Canadian fintech competition got minimal airtime.

**BMO** — quietly the most competitive on product, least vocal about it. InvestorLine has offered commission-free ETFs for years (broader list than RBC's), Smartfolio was the first big bank robo. But no strategic positioning against Wealthsimple. Focus is on U.S. expansion (Bank of the West acquisition).

**Scotiabank** — focused internationally. Tangerine should be their natural weapon — it's already a digital-first, fee-free brand. But it's been neglected. No self-directed investing, basic credit cards, stale product set.

**CIBC** — the most exposed, the least responsive. Most Canada-concentrated of the Big Five with the smallest wealth management platform. Investor's Edge is the only major bank platform that still doesn't offer any commission-free ETFs. Simplii Financial hasn't evolved materially.

**National Bank** — the only one that moved first. Went fully commission-free on all stocks and ETFs back in 2021 — years before anyone else. The smallest of the Big Six, which explains the urgency the larger banks don't seem to feel.

## The Meta-Observation

No Big Six CEO has stood up at an investor day and said: "Wealthsimple is taking our under-40 clients at an accelerating rate, here is our specific plan to compete." The closest is RBC's personal banking head acknowledging they're monitoring fintech disruption "in a very material way." Monitoring isn't a strategy.

The Big Six treat fintech competition as a risk factor disclosure item — listed alongside cybersecurity, foreign exchange, and climate risk — rather than the specific structural challenge it is.

The banks' collective posture is: "We have scale, branches, lending relationships, and regulatory moats — fintechs can have the low-margin, fee-sensitive clients." That may be a rational near-term capital allocation decision. But it's the same logic that led Blockbuster to cede streaming to Netflix: rational quarter-by-quarter, catastrophic on a 10-year horizon.

The window for a proactive strategic response is narrowing, and the silence from bank leadership is becoming more conspicuous each quarter.
